100. General
100.1. These rules are effective as of October 6, 2023.
100.1a. A rule marked with a star (*) is a gameplay rule.
100.1b. A rule marked with a dagger (â€ ) is a tournament rule.
100.2. The Magic: The Gathering trading card game is a game of strategy and skill.
100.2a. It is played by two or more players, each with a deck of cards.
100.2b. The goal of the game is to reduce your opponent's life total to 0.
100.3. The cards in the game are divided into five colors: white, blue, black, red, and green.
100.3a. Each color has its own strengths and weaknesses.
100.3b. There are also colorless cards, which can be played in any deck.
100.4. The game is played in a series of turns.
100.4a. Each turn has five phases: beginning, precombat main, combat, postcombat main, and ending.
100.4b. Each phase is further divided into steps.
100.5. The game uses a stack to resolve spells and abilities.
100.5a. When a player casts a spell or activates an ability, it goes on the stack.
100.5b. Players can respond to spells and abilities on the stack by casting their own spells or activating their own abilities.
100.6. The game has a number of keywords that represent common abilities.
100.6a. These keywords include flying, trample, and first strike.
100.6b. A complete list of keywords can be found in the glossary.
200. The Game
200.1. The game of Magic is played by two or more players.
200.1a. The players start with a certain amount of life.
200.1b. The default amount of life is 20.
200.2. The game is played with a deck of cards.
200.2a. The minimum deck size is 60 cards.
200.2b. There is no maximum deck size, but you must be able to shuffle your deck without assistance.
200.3. The game is played on a playmat.
200.3a. The playmat is divided into a number of zones.
200.3b. These zones include the library, hand, battlefield, graveyard, and exile.
300. Game Concepts
300.1. The game has a number of concepts that are important to understand.
300.1a. These concepts include the stack, priority, and phases.
300.1b. A complete list of game concepts can be found in the glossary.
300.2. The stack is a zone where spells and abilities wait to resolve.
300.2a. When a player casts a spell or activates an ability, it goes on top of the stack.
300.2b. The topmost object on the stack resolves first.
300.3. Priority is the right to cast spells and activate abilities.
300.3a. The active player gets priority at the beginning of most steps and phases.
300.3b. When a player has priority, they can either cast a spell, activate an ability, or pass priority to the next player.
300.4. Phases are the main divisions of a turn.
300.4a. There are five phases in a turn: beginning, precombat main, combat, postcombat main, and ending.
300.4b. Each phase is further divided into steps.
400. Zones
400.1. There are a number of zones in the game.
400.1a. These zones include the library, hand, battlefield, graveyard, and exile.
400.1b. A complete list of zones can be found in the glossary.
400.2. The library is where a player's deck is kept.
400.2a. The cards in the library are face down and in a random order.
400.2b. Players can't look at the cards in their library unless a spell or ability allows them to.
400.3. The hand is where a player keeps the cards they've drawn.
400.3a. The cards in a player's hand are hidden from other players.
400.3b. A player can have a maximum of seven cards in their hand at the end of their turn. If they have more than seven, they must discard down to seven.
400.4. The battlefield is where permanents are put when they're played.
400.4a. Permanents on the battlefield are face up and visible to all players.
400.4b. There is no limit to the number of permanents a player can have on the battlefield.
400.5. The graveyard is where cards go when they're destroyed or discarded.
400.5a. The cards in the graveyard are face up and visible to all players.
400.5b. The order of cards in the graveyard can't be changed unless a spell or ability allows it.
400.6. Exile is a zone where cards are put when they're exiled.
400.6a. The cards in exile are face up and visible to all players.
400.6b. Cards in exile are generally considered to be outside the game.
500. Turn Structure
500.1. A turn consists of five phases, in this order: beginning, precombat main, combat, postcombat main, and ending.
500.1a. Each of these phases is further divided into steps.
500.1b. A complete list of phases and steps can be found in the glossary.
500.2. The beginning phase consists of three steps: untap, upkeep, and draw.
500.2a. During the untap step, the active player untaps all of their tapped permanents.
500.2b. During the upkeep step, any abilities that trigger at the beginning of the upkeep go on the stack.
500.2c. During the draw step, the active player draws a card.
500.3. The precombat main phase is when the active player can cast spells and play lands.
500.3a. The active player has priority during the precombat main phase.
500.3b. The active player can cast any type of spell during the precombat main phase.
500.4. The combat phase consists of five steps: beginning of combat, declare attackers, declare blockers, combat damage, and end of combat.
500.4a. During the beginning of combat step, any abilities that trigger at the beginning of combat go on the stack.
500.4b. During the declare attackers step, the active player declares which of their creatures are attacking.
500.4c. During the declare blockers step, the defending player declares which of their creatures are blocking.
500.4d. During the combat damage step, creatures deal damage to each other.
500.4e. During the end of combat step, any abilities that trigger at the end of combat go on the stack.
500.5. The postcombat main phase is another opportunity for the active player to cast spells and play lands.
500.5a. The active player has priority during the postcombat main phase.
500.5b. The active player can cast any type of spell during the postcombat main phase.
500.6. The ending phase consists of two steps: end and cleanup.
500.6a. During the end step, any abilities that trigger at the beginning of the end step go on the stack.
500.6b. During the cleanup step, the active player discards down to their maximum hand size, and all damage is removed from creatures.
600. Spells, Abilities, and Effects
600.1. Spells and abilities can be put on the stack.
600.1a. Effects are the result of spells and abilities.
600.1b. A complete list of spells, abilities, and effects can be found in the glossary.
600.2. A spell is a card that a player casts from their hand.
600.2a. When a player casts a spell, it goes on top of the stack.
600.2b. The spell resolves when all players have passed priority in succession.
600.3. An ability is a characteristic of a card that can be activated or triggered.
600.3a. An activated ability is an ability that a player can activate by paying a cost.
600.3b. A triggered ability is an ability that triggers automatically when a certain event occurs.
600.4. An effect is something that happens in the game as the result of a spell or ability.
600.4a. Effects can be continuous or one-shot.
600.4b. A continuous effect is an effect that lasts for a certain duration.
600.4c. A one-shot effect is an effect that happens once and then is done.
700. Additional Rules
700.1. There are a number of additional rules that apply to the game.
700.1a. These rules include the rules for mulligans, concessions, and loops.
700.1b. A complete list of additional rules can be found in the glossary.
700.2. A mulligan is when a player chooses to shuffle their hand back into their library and draw a new hand.
700.2a. A player can take a mulligan as many times as they want, but each time they draw one fewer card.
700.2b. The first mulligan is a "free" mulligan, meaning the player draws a full hand of seven cards.
700.3. A concession is when a player chooses to forfeit the game.
700.3a. A player can concede at any time, even when they don't have priority.
700.3b. When a player concedes, they lose the game.
700.4. A loop is a sequence of events that repeats over and over again.
700.4a. If a loop is discovered, the game enters a state-based action check.
700.4b. If the loop is still present after the state-based action check, the game is a draw.
800. Multiplayer Rules
800.1. The multiplayer rules apply to games with more than two players.
800.1a. These rules include the rules for Two-Headed Giant, Commander, and Archenemy.
800.1b. A complete list of multiplayer rules can be found in the glossary.
800.2. Two-Headed Giant is a format where two teams of two players play against each other.
800.2a. Each team shares a life total of 30.
800.2b. Each team takes their turn together.
800.3. Commander is a format where each player has a commander, which is a legendary creature that can be cast from the command zone.
800.3a. The commander's color identity determines which cards can be included in the deck.
800.3b. Each player starts with 40 life.
800.4. Archenemy is a format where three players team up to play against one "archenemy" player, who has a deck of powerful scheme cards.
800.4a. The archenemy starts with 40 life.
800.4b. The archenemy's teammates start with 20 life each.
900. Casual Play
900.1. The casual play rules apply to games that are not part of a tournament.
900.1a. These rules include the rules for Planechase, Vanguard, and Conspiracy Draft.
900.1b. A complete list of casual play rules can be found in the glossary.
900.2. Planechase is a format where players travel to different planes, each with its own unique effect on the game.
900.2a. Players use a deck of oversized plane cards.
900.2b. At the beginning of each player's turn, they roll a special planar die to see if they travel to a new plane.
900.3. Vanguard is a format where each player has a vanguard card, which represents a powerful character with unique abilities.
900.3a. The vanguard card starts the game in the command zone.
900.3b. The vanguard card's abilities can be used throughout the game.
900.4. Conspiracy Draft is a format where players draft cards from special Conspiracy sets, which include cards that affect the draft itself.
900.4a. Players can also draft special conspiracy cards, which have powerful effects on the game.
900.4b. The draft is followed by a multiplayer game where players use the decks they drafted.
